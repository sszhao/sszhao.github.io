1.1 What is HCI?

Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) stands at the intersection of computer science, behavioral sciences, and design. It is not merely about making computers easier to use, but about understanding the profound relationship between human cognition and computational systems.

1.1.1 Defining Human-Computer Interaction

Paul Dourish, a seminal figure in the field, describes HCI not as a subfield of computer science, but as a discipline that explores how people interact with technological artifacts.

"Interaction is not just a dialogue between a human and a computer; it is the enactment of social and cognitive practices through the medium of the interface."

"

In this light, HCI research focuses on the novelty of interaction techniques and the creation of scientific knowledge that informs the next generation of computing paradigms.

1.1.2 Distinguishing HCI Research from UI/UX Design

A common misconception is that HCI research and UI/UX design are synonymous. While they share tools and methods, their primary goals diverge significantly.

HCI RESEARCH

Focused on generalizable knowledge, theoretical frameworks, and exploring "what could be" through scientific rigor.

UI/UX DESIGN

Focused on specific product success, user satisfaction, and business metrics within existing market constraints.

Where a designer seeks to optimize a specific flow, the researcher seeks to understand the underlying principles of the interaction itself. This distinction is crucial for scholars navigating the academic landscape where novelty is the currency of publication.

The Scholarly Mindset

When documenting your contributions, focus on the 'Knowledge Delta'—what does the world know now that it didn't know before your study? This distinguishes research from craft.
